The Mock Battle:

After the Philippines, Zhare, and Canadian delegations arrived at the designated time and location, the announcers began by outlining the rules:

  • Only medieval weaponry would be used.
  • No guns, ammunition, or modern weapons were allowed.
  • Soldiers would be considered "down" if they were touched by a wooden sword or spear.
  • Winning, losing, and stalemate conditions were clearly defined
  • In the event of a stalemate, a second round would be conducted.

Both teams were given five minutes to set up their camps and strategic positions within the five-hectare battlefield. Spectators, equipped with cameras and drones, were ready to observe the battle with neutrality.

At the end of the prep time, an announcement signaled the battle’s imminent start. In a scene reminiscent of Squid Game, the legions (Zhare, Brethren, and the Kingdoms) and battalions (British forces) stood at the ready. With the sound of a starting gun, the legions swiftly moved to secure key locations, while the battalions initially remained stationed at their main camp.

Upon receiving orders from their generals, the battalions advanced but were repelled by the heavily fortified positions of the legions. This resulted in minimal casualties for the legions, who won the first engagements.

As time passed, the legions at their main camp pushed forward, capturing strategic points. Some legions launched a direct assault on the battalion's main camp, while others secured flags at key locations before returning to reinforce the main attack.

As the battle raged on, it became clear that victory belonged to the legions. Critics compared the Zharian battle strategy to the Roman conquest of Gaul, while others disagreed. However, most military experts present acknowledged its Roman-style combat tactics.

By the end of the battle:

  • 90% of the British battalions were eliminated.
  • The legions remained at full strength.
  • The victory was decisively awarded to Zhare's Legions.

Following the mock battle, Zharian ships arrived at the Canadian borders, accompanied by fighter jets soaring through Canadian airspace. Massive transport ships—some appearing to have anti-gravity modules—descended near the battlefield. These airborne vessels demonstrated Zhare’s technological advancements, leading many to compare them to the Type 3 Interstellar Civilization on the Kardashev scale.

Observers noted that Zhare’s transport fleet was:

  • Capable of rapid deployment.
  • Independently transporting its forces.
  • Not reliant on British military ships for the return journey.

Each transport ship carried soldiers back to their respective Kingdoms:

  • Mecka Legion returned to Mecka Kingdom.
  • Other legions were similarly transported to their homelands.

Meanwhile, another Zharian transport ship was dispatched to Canada to retrieve the Philippine delegation, bringing them home via a landing at Malacañang Palace.

This display of Zhare’s military and logistical capabilities sent a strong message to the world—that Zhare was ready to defend its interests.

In celebration, Zhare’s air force launched flares and fireworks, while its navy—including destroyers, frigates, and coast guard ships—fired missiles into the sky, detonating them as a symbolic victory display.

Despite Britain offering to transport Zhare and the Philippines back via military ships, Zharian vessels had already arrived independently, even navigating the Panama Canal effortlessly and traversing South America’s coastline to reach Canada

For clarity:

  • Zharian transport ships were airborne—akin to cargo planes but vastly more advanced.
  • Zharian naval ships (destroyers, frigates, and coast guards) remained on water
  • Flares served as a fireworks display by Zhare’s air force.

This mock battle and its aftermath solidified Zhare’s growing influence, proving that even a micronation could stand as a global force when properly equipped and led.
